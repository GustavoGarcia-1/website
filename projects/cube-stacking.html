Cube Stacking Robot

Overview

This project benchmarks multiple vision-language-action architectures on a cube stacking task using the so101 robotic arms. I trained and compared three models — smolVLA, ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers), and π0.5 — on a shared teleoperation dataset to understand their relative strengths and failure modes for precise pick-and-place manipulation.

Training was distributed across H100 GPUs via VESSL AI, Google Colab, and the San Diego Supercomputer Center (SDCC). All checkpoints were uploaded to HuggingFace for reproducibility and community access.

Data Collection & Preprocessing

Hours of teleoperation data were collected on the so101 arms, with the operator performing cube stacking sequences of varying difficulty (single stack, multi-stack, different starting positions). Each episode was recorded with synchronized multi-view cameras to provide both top-down and wrist-level perspectives.

Data was preprocessed into the LeRobot dataset format with consistent frame rates and action representations across all training runs to ensure fair comparison between model architectures.

Model Comparison

Each model was trained on the same dataset with architecture-specific hyperparameters tuned on a validation split:

smolVLA — A lightweight VLA model designed for efficiency. It uses a compact vision encoder and smaller language backbone, making it faster to train and run inference on but potentially sacrificing representation capacity.

ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) — Predicts chunks of future actions using a CVAE encoder-decoder architecture. ACT's action chunking approach helps with temporal consistency, reducing the compounding error problem in sequential prediction.

π0.5 — Physical Intelligence's flow matching policy that generates continuous action trajectories. Its flow-based formulation allows for smooth, precise motions well-suited to stacking tasks.

# Example: Training ACT with LeRobot
python lerobot/scripts/train.py \
    policy=act \
    env=so101_cube_stacking \
    dataset_repo_id=gustavo/cube-stacking-so101 \
    training.num_epochs=300 \
    training.batch_size=64 \
    wandb.enable=true

Training Infrastructure & Results

Training was distributed across multiple compute platforms. VESSL AI and SDCC provided H100 GPU access for the larger models (π0.5), while Google Colab was used for lighter experiments and ablations with smolVLA and ACT. All runs were tracked with Weights & Biases for consistent metric comparison.

Each model's checkpoints were uploaded to HuggingFace, making it straightforward to load and evaluate any checkpoint for real-world rollouts on the so101 arms.

The comparison revealed trade-offs between model size, training compute, and task success rate that informed our team's architecture choices for future manipulation tasks at Triton Droids.
